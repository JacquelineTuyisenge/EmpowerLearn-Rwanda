menstrual Health and circle

Menstruation:

Menstruation, or period, is normal vaginal bleeding that occurs as part of a woman's monthly cycle. Every month, your body prepares for pregnancy. If no pregnancy occurs, the uterus, or womb, sheds its lining.


In our Rwandan culture, Menstruation was seen as taboo, where there were certain activivities girls and women were not allowed to do while they were in it like: carrying babies, adding salt in food, and others


Breaking the Stigma: Embracing Menstruation

At Breaking Barriers, we celebrate menstruation as a natural and healthy aspect of life. It signifies good health and the incredible ability to bring life into the world. Let's shift the narrative, menstruation is not a taboo but a symbol of strength and vitality. It's a sign that you are healthy and capable of nurturing life. Let's take pride in this natural process and create a supportive environment where open conversations about menstruation are encouraged, celebrated, and embraced.

Tracking Menstrual Cycles: A Simple Guide

For Regular Cycles:

Start Tracking: Begin by marking the first day of your period on a calendar(That is the first day of yur circle)

Count Days: Continue marking until the next period, the day before next period is the last day of your circle so you count those days. menstrual cycle ranges from 21 to 35 days, with the average being around 28 days. try this for several months and do the average, then that is days of your circle

Ovulation Prediction: Around the middle of your cycle (approximately 14 days before your next period), you're likely to ovulate. This is the best time for conception if you're planning for pregnancy.

Symptom Monitoring: Note any physical or emotional changes, like mood swings or physical discomfort, throughout your cycle. It helps understand your body's patterns.

Tracking Menstrual Cycles: A Simple Guide

For Irregular Cycles:

Observation: Even with irregular cycles, tracking is helpful. Start marking the first day of your period as day one each cycle.

Record Variations: Note the duration and patterns of each cycle. This can help identify trends or irregularities over time. keep doing that for atleast six months then you will do average to find out the range of your cycle.

Self-Care: Prioritize self-care during menstruation with adequate rest, hydration, and managing discomfort. Remember, every person's menstrual cycle is unique. Regular tracking helps in understanding individual patterns and seeking necessary support or advice if needed.

Addressing Gender-Based Violence:

Gender-based violence refers to any type of harm that is perpetrated against a person or group of people because of their factual or perceived sex, gender

There are four main Types of GBV:

  • Physical Violence: refers to any act causing physical harm or injury, such as hitting, punching, or restraining someone based on their gender.
  • Sexual Violence: Non-consensual sexual acts, harassment, assault, or exploitation, touch,..
  • Emotional/Psychological Violence: Controlling behaviors, threats, intimidation, or verbal abuse causing emotional harm and hateful speech.
  • Economic Violence: Financial control, deprivation of resources, or employment-related discrimination.

Combatting Gender-Based Violence:

  • Raising Awareness: By making people aware of the violence, awakes them to start seeing the truth.
  • Recognizing Signs: Educating individuals to identify signs of violence, empowering them to seek help and support.
  • Support for Survivors: Creating safe spaces for survivors to share their experiences, access counseling services, and connect with support networks. Listening to them and help them reach the one who can help them.

Effects of Gender-Based Violence:

GBV leaves profound and long-lasting effects on individuals, causing physical injuries, mental health issues like anxiety and depression, trauma, and emotional distress. It disrupts lives, perpetuates cycles of violence, and hampers individual growth and societal progress.


Breaking Barriers stands firm in advocating for an end to GBV through education, support, and active efforts to create a society free from violence and discrimination. Together, we can combat GBV by promoting respect, equality, and creating safe spaces for all individuals.
